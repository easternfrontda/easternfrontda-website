What I Wish Every New Gun Owner Knew (Before Their First Range Day)

Matthew Stanley March 2025 7 min read

Congratulations—you've just purchased your first firearm. Maybe it's for home defense, concealed carry, or simply because you want to exercise your Second Amendment rights. Whatever the reason, you're probably feeling a mix of excitement and nervousness about your first trip to the shooting range.

Over 13 years of firearms instruction—from military training to teaching thousands of civilians—I've worked with hundreds of first-time gun owners. I've seen the same questions, the same concerns, and unfortunately, the same preventable mistakes over and over again.

This article is what I wish I could tell every new gun owner before they head to the range for the first time. Following this advice will make your experience safer, more productive, and far less stressful.

1. Everyone Was a Beginner Once (Including Me)

The first thing you need to know: it's okay to be nervous. It's okay to not know what you're doing. It's okay to feel a bit intimidated.

Despite my 13 years of experience, I still remember my first time handling a firearm. I was nervous. My hands shook a little. I second-guessed every movement. That's completely normal and actually a good sign—it means you respect the responsibility you're taking on.

"The best shooters aren't the ones who were never scared. They're the ones who learned despite being scared."

Here's what experienced shooters know that beginners don't: nobody is judging you. The gun community is generally welcoming to new shooters. Most people at the range are focused on their own shooting, and those who do notice you're new are typically eager to help, not criticize.

If you're taking a professional course (which I strongly recommend), the instructor's entire job is to help you learn safely and confidently. There are no stupid questions in firearms training—only questions that should be asked.

2. Safety Isn't Optional—It's Everything

Before we talk about anything else, let's establish the foundation: the four fundamental rules of firearms safety. These aren't suggestions. They're non-negotiable principles that must be followed 100% of the time.

The Four Rules (Memorize These)

  • Treat every firearm as if it's loaded - Even if you just checked it, even if someone told you it's empty, treat it as loaded. Always.
  • Never point the muzzle at anything you're not willing to destroy - Keep it pointed in a safe direction at all times. At the range, that's downrange. Everywhere else, that's usually down at the ground or up at the ceiling.
  • Keep your finger off the trigger until your sights are on target and you're ready to fire - Your trigger finger should rest along the frame, not on the trigger, until the moment you're actually shooting.
  • Be sure of your target and what's beyond it - Bullets don't stop just because they hit your intended target. Know what's behind it.

Follow these four rules religiously, and you will never have a negligent discharge. Break even one of them, and people can get hurt or killed. It's that simple.

At the range, there will also be specific range rules (when you can go downrange, when you can handle your firearm, etc.). Follow them exactly. Range safety officers aren't being difficult—they're keeping everyone alive.

3. You Don't Need Expensive Gear (Yet)

One of the biggest mistakes new gun owners make is spending a fortune on gear before they even know what they need.

You don't need:

  • A $2,000 custom pistol
  • A $500 holster system
  • Every accessory in the gun store
  • Enough ammunition to survive the apocalypse

What you actually need for your first range day:

Essential Gear Checklist

  • Your firearm (obviously)
  • 2-3 magazines (if applicable)
  • Eye protection - ANSI Z87.1 rated safety glasses (not regular sunglasses). Around $10-30.
  • Ear protection - Foam earplugs ($5) or earmuffs ($20-50). Electronic earmuffs are nice but not necessary.
  • 50-100 rounds of ammunition - Start small. You'll learn more from 50 focused rounds than 500 rushed ones.
  • A case or bag to transport your firearm safely

That's it. Everything else can wait until you've actually shot your firearm and know what you like and don't like.

Pro Tip: Buy Once, Cry Once

When you do invest in gear, buy quality basics rather than cheap everything. A $400 Glock will outlast a $200 off-brand pistol. Good eye and ear protection is worth the investment. But don't confuse "quality" with "expensive"—you don't need the tactical operator deluxe edition of everything.

4. Your First Shots Will Probably Be Terrible (And That's Okay)

Here's what's going to happen on your first range day: your shots will be all over the target. Maybe some won't even hit the target. Your hands will shake. You'll flinch. Your groups will look like a shotgun pattern.

This is completely normal. I've never met a new shooter who produced perfect groups on their first day. Not one.

Why beginners struggle:

  • Anticipation/Flinching: You're subconsciously bracing for the recoil and noise, causing you to jerk the gun before it fires
  • Grip issues: Too loose, too tight, improper hand placement
  • Trigger control: Slapping or jerking the trigger instead of smoothly pressing it
  • Vision focus: Looking at the target instead of the front sight
  • Breathing: Holding your breath or breathing at the wrong time

All of these are fixable with practice and instruction. The key is to not get discouraged. Marksmanship is a skill that takes time to develop.

Realistic First-Day Expectations:

Success looks like:

  • All shots hit the paper (even if they're not in the bullseye)
  • You follow all safety rules
  • You understand how your firearm operates
  • You're more confident than when you started
  • You want to come back and practice more

Success does NOT require:

  • Tight groups
  • Bullseye hits
  • Looking like an action movie hero
  • Impressing anyone

5. Start Close and Slow

One of the most common mistakes I see: new shooters set up at 25 yards and wonder why they can't hit anything.

Start at 3-5 yards. Seriously. That's about 10-15 feet from the target.

"But isn't that too close?" No. Here's why:

  • Most civilian defensive encounters happen at under 7 yards
  • You need to build confidence and good habits first
  • You can actually see what you're doing right or wrong
  • It's easier to learn fundamentals when you're not frustrated by missed shots

Once you can consistently hit center mass at 3-5 yards, move back to 7 yards. Then 10. Then 15. There's no rush.

Also shoot slowly. One shot every 3-5 seconds when you're starting. Focus on doing everything correctly, not on speed. Speed comes naturally with practice. Bad habits developed in the name of speed are hard to break.

6. Ammunition Matters More Than You Think

For your first few range trips, use standard target ammunition (often labeled "FMJ" or "Full Metal Jacket"). It's cheaper and produces less recoil than defensive ammunition.

Don't buy the cheapest ammunition you can find. Stick with reputable brands:

  • Federal
  • Winchester
  • Remington
  • CCI/Blazer
  • Fiocchi

Cheap, poorly-made ammunition causes malfunctions, which is frustrating and interrupts your learning. Save $5 on a box of ammo, spend an hour dealing with jams—not a good trade.

How much to bring: Start with 50 rounds. If you're taking a class, the instructor will tell you how much to bring (usually 100-200 rounds depending on the course).

7. Consider Professional Instruction (Seriously)

I'm obviously biased here, but after 13 years of teaching firearms, I can tell you: professional instruction accelerates your learning dramatically.

Here's why:

  • You learn correct techniques from the start instead of developing bad habits you'll have to unlearn later
  • Immediate feedback on what you're doing right and wrong
  • Safety oversight so you can focus on learning instead of worrying about making a dangerous mistake
  • Structured progression instead of just shooting at targets randomly
  • Confidence from knowing an experienced instructor is guiding you

Can you teach yourself? Sure. People do it all the time. But it's slower, more frustrating, and you might develop bad habits that will take years to fix.

The YouTube Problem

YouTube is full of firearms instruction videos. Some are excellent. Many are terrible. The problem? As a beginner, you can't tell the difference. What looks authoritative might actually be teaching you dangerous or ineffective techniques. Professional instruction gives you a trusted source of information from someone who actually knows what they're doing.

8. Malfunctions Will Happen (Don't Panic)

At some point during your shooting journey—maybe even on your first day—your firearm will malfunction. The round won't chamber, the case won't eject, something will go wrong.

This is normal. All firearms malfunction eventually.

When it happens:

  1. Keep the firearm pointed downrange
  2. Keep your finger off the trigger
  3. If you're not sure what to do, call for help

If you're taking a class, the instructor will show you how to clear common malfunctions. If you're at a public range, the range safety officer can help. Never try to force anything—if the gun won't operate normally, get assistance.

Malfunctions aren't failures. They're learning opportunities.

9. Dry Fire Practice: Your Secret Weapon

Here's something that will dramatically accelerate your progress: dry fire practice.

Dry firing means practicing with an unloaded firearm at home. No ammunition, no range fees, no noise—just you, your gun, and focused repetition of proper technique.

Why Dry Fire Is So Powerful

Most shooters only practice during live fire at the range. That means they're practicing maybe once or twice a month. But with dry fire, you can practice daily—building muscle memory and perfecting technique far faster than live fire alone ever could.

Think about it: 10 minutes of daily dry fire at home gives you 70 minutes per week of trigger time. Even if you only go to the range monthly, you're getting more practice time through dry fire than most shooters get all year.

What You Can Practice with Dry Fire

  • Trigger control: Smooth, steady squeeze without disturbing sight alignment
  • Sight alignment: Proper front sight focus
  • Grip consistency: Same grip every single time
  • Drawing from holster: Smooth, safe draw stroke (advanced)
  • Reloads: Magazine changes and manipulation
  • Malfunction clearances: Tap-rack drills
  • Presentation: Bringing the gun from ready to target

How to Dry Fire Safely

Safety is absolutely critical when dry firing. Follow this procedure religiously:

  1. Choose a dedicated practice space with a safe backstop (an exterior wall is ideal)
  2. Remove ALL ammunition from the room - Put it in another room entirely, not just across the room
  3. Physically check your firearm - Visually inspect the chamber, run your finger through it, confirm it's completely empty
  4. Check again - No, seriously. Check it again. Every time you pick it up, even if you just checked it 30 seconds ago
  5. Choose a specific target - A small spot on the wall, a light switch, anything that gives you a precise aiming point
  6. Never point the gun anywhere unsafe - Even unloaded, maintain muzzle discipline
  7. When you're done, check it again before putting it away

⚠️ Critical Safety Note

Every single negligent discharge during dry fire practice happens the same way: someone brought live ammunition into their practice space or didn't properly clear their firearm. Follow the safety protocol above without exception. No shortcuts. Ever.

A Simple Dry Fire Routine for New Shooters

Here's a 10-minute daily routine that will dramatically improve your shooting:

  1. Clear your firearm (chamber, magazine, check again)
  2. Remove all ammunition from the room
  3. 5 minutes: Trigger control
    • Focus on front sight
    • Smooth, straight-back trigger squeeze
    • Front sight shouldn't move when trigger breaks
    • Repeat 20-30 times
  4. 3 minutes: Grip and presentation
    • Start at low ready (gun pointed down)
    • Present to target
    • Acquire sight picture
    • Press trigger
    • Repeat 15-20 times
  5. 2 minutes: Reloads
    • Practice magazine changes
    • Empty magazines are fine for this
    • Focus on smooth, consistent motion
    • Repeat 10-15 times

Do this daily for 30 days and you'll see more improvement than most shooters achieve in a year of occasional range trips.

Will Dry Firing Damage My Gun?

This is a common concern. The answer: it depends on the firearm.

  • Modern centerfire pistols (Glock, Sig, Smith & Wesson M&P, etc.): Perfectly safe to dry fire. They're designed for it.
  • Modern centerfire rifles (AR-15, AK, bolt actions): Generally safe to dry fire.
  • .22 rimfire firearms: Should NOT be dry fired without snap caps (dummy rounds that protect the firing pin).
  • Older firearms or uncertain models: Use snap caps to be safe.

Snap caps cost $10-20 and protect your firearm during dry fire practice. If you're unsure about your specific firearm, use snap caps or check your owner's manual.

The Bottom Line on Dry Fire

I cannot overstate how important dry fire practice is. It's the difference between mediocre shooters and skilled shooters. Every professional—military, law enforcement, competitive shooters—practices dry fire extensively.

It's free (after you buy snap caps if needed). It's convenient (do it at home in your pajamas). It's quiet (won't disturb neighbors). And it works.

If you take only one piece of advice from this entire article, make it this: dry fire practice, done safely and consistently, will make you a better shooter faster than anything else you can do.

10. Practice Matters More Than Gear

The gun community has an equipment obsession. People spend thousands on custom guns, fancy holsters, red dot sights, and tactical gear, then shoot 50 rounds a year.

This is backwards.

A skilled shooter with a $400 Glock will outperform a novice with a $3,000 custom pistol. Every. Single. Time.

"Don't practice until you get it right. Practice until you can't get it wrong."

For a new shooter, I recommend:

  • First 3 months: Range time every 2 weeks (50-100 rounds per session)
  • Months 3-6: Monthly range time (100-200 rounds per session)
  • After 6 months: Quarterly range time minimum to maintain skills

Consistent practice beats expensive gear. Always.

11. Know When to Take a Break

Here's something nobody tells new shooters: mental fatigue happens before physical fatigue.

You'll notice your shots getting worse after 30-45 minutes. You'll start making mistakes. Your concentration will slip. This isn't weakness—it's normal.

When you notice performance degrading:

  • Take a 10-15 minute break
  • Hydrate (shooting is surprisingly dehydrating)
  • Step away from the firing line
  • Reset your mind

Pushing through fatigue doesn't make you tougher—it reinforces bad habits. Quality practice beats quantity every time.

12. Respect the Responsibility

Owning a firearm is a serious responsibility. It's not just about knowing how to shoot—it's about:

  • Safe storage: Preventing unauthorized access (especially children)
  • Legal knowledge: Understanding when use of force is justified
  • Ongoing training: Maintaining your skills
  • Mental preparedness: Understanding the psychological reality of using a firearm for self-defense

Firearms are tools that demand respect and responsibility. The gun community has high standards because the consequences of negligence can be fatal.

Take this responsibility seriously, and you'll be welcomed into a community of responsible gun owners who are passionate about safety, training, and the Second Amendment.

Final Thoughts: You've Got This

Your first range day might feel overwhelming. You might be nervous. You might not shoot as well as you'd hoped. That's all okay.

Every experienced shooter you see at the range was once exactly where you are now. The difference between them and you is just time and practice.

Follow safety rules religiously. Start slow and close. Get professional instruction if possible. Practice consistently. Be patient with yourself.

Within a few months, you'll look back at this article and smile, remembering how nervous you were—and how far you've come.
