What 10,000 Ukrainian Soldiers Taught Me About Firearms Training

Matthew Stanley March 2025 8 min read

Between 2022 and 2025, I had the privilege of training over 10,000 soldiers and civilians in Ukraine—many of whom went directly to the front lines in Kharkiv, Zaporizhia, and other contested regions. I didn't just teach from behind a safe training facility. I fought alongside them, conducted operations with them, and saw firsthand which techniques worked under fire and which ones got people killed.

Now that I'm bringing that experience to civilian firearms training in Fort Wayne through Eastern Front Defense Academy, I want to share the most important lessons I learned—lessons that apply just as much to a homeowner defending their family as they did to soldiers in a combat zone.

"The best training isn't what looks cool on a square range. It's what keeps you alive when everything goes wrong."

Lesson 1: Simple Always Beats Complex

When I first arrived in Ukraine, I expected to teach advanced tactical maneuvers, complex reloading techniques, and sophisticated combat strategies. What I quickly discovered was that soldiers under stress revert to their most basic training.

I watched highly trained soldiers forget complicated reload sequences during their first firefight. I saw intricate room-clearing techniques fall apart the moment bullets started flying. The fighters who survived and succeeded weren't the ones with the most advanced techniques—they were the ones who had mastered the fundamentals so completely that they became automatic.

What This Means for Civilian Training:

Stop chasing the latest "tactical" techniques you see on YouTube. Master these fundamentals first:

  • Grip: A consistent, repeatable grip you can execute in the dark, under stress, when all fine motor skills dissipate
  • Trigger control: Smooth squeeze without disturbing sight alignment, every single time
  • Sight alignment: Front sight focus that you can maintain even when your body is flooded with adrenaline
  • Reloads: One reload technique practiced 1,000 times beats five techniques practiced 200 times each
  • Malfunction clearance: One immediate action drill, burned into muscle memory

In my courses, we drill these fundamentals until they're boring. Then we drill them some more. Because when your life depends on it, boring and reliable beats exciting and unreliable every single time.

Lesson 2: Most People Have Never Actually Been Stressed

Here's something that surprised me: most civilian firearms instructors have never experienced genuine life-threatening stress. They've never been shot at. They've never had to make split-second decisions while bullets are snapping past their head. They've never felt real fear and had to fight through it.

This matters because stress changes everything. Your fine motor skills degrade. Your vision narrows. Your hands shake. Your breathing becomes rapid and shallow. Time seems to speed up or slow down. And all those perfect techniques you practiced on a calm Sunday afternoon? They can vanish.

The Stress Performance Gap

Studies show that performance under stress can drop by 50-70% for skills that haven't been properly trained. This is why military and law enforcement spend so much time on stress inoculation training—they need to close this performance gap before lives depend on it.

What I Learned in Ukraine:

Soldiers who had experienced stress inoculation training—training that deliberately introduced stress, confusion, and pressure—performed dramatically better in actual combat. They weren't unaffected by stress (nobody is), but they had learned to function through it.

The fighters who only trained on calm, controlled ranges? Many froze, panicked, or made fatal mistakes the first time they came under fire.

How I Apply This in My Courses:

I progressively introduce stress into training:

  1. Time pressure: "You have 3 seconds to engage these targets"
  2. Physical exertion: Shoot after elevated heart rate (burpees, sprints)
  3. Unexpected scenarios: Unknown number of targets, surprise malfunctions
  4. Decision-making pressure: Shoot/no-shoot scenarios with consequences
  5. Environmental challenges: Low light, awkward positions, moving platforms

This isn't about making training "tactical" or "cool." It's about preparing you for the reality that when you need your firearm, you'll be scared, stressed, and operating at reduced capacity. My job is to make sure your reduced capacity is still good enough to save your life.

Lesson 3: Equipment Doesn't Matter as Much as You Think

In Ukraine, I saw soldiers with custom pistols get outperformed by fighters using beat-up Soviet-era Makarovs. I watched guys with all the latest gear freeze under fire, while soldiers with basic equipment executed flawlessly.

The Western firearms community has developed an obsession with equipment: the "best" pistol, the "perfect" optic, the "ideal" ammunition. Don't get me wrong—quality equipment matters. But I learned that a mediocre shooter with expensive gear is still a mediocre shooter.

"I'd rather have a $400 Glock and 1,000 hours of training than a $4,000 custom pistol and 100 hours of training."

What You Actually Need:

  • A reliable firearm from a major manufacturer (Glock, Smith & Wesson, Sig, etc.)
  • Good quality ammunition (doesn't need to be premium defensive ammo for training)
  • Proper eye and ear protection (ANSI rated, comfortable for long sessions)
  • A decent holster (secure retention, consistent draw)
  • Enough magazines (at least 3, so you can train reloads properly)

That's it. Everything else is nice-to-have. Spend your money on ammunition and training, not on the newest tacti-cool accessories.

Lesson 4: Most Gunfights Are Decided Before the First Shot

This was perhaps the most important lesson from Ukraine: the outcome of most engagements is determined by awareness, positioning, and decision-making—not marksmanship.

The fighters who survived weren't necessarily the best shots. They were the ones who:

  • Saw the threat first (situational awareness)
  • Used cover effectively (didn't stand in the open like a target)
  • Made smart tactical decisions (when to engage, when to withdraw)
  • Positioned themselves advantageously (attacking from unexpected angles)
  • Controlled the initiative (acted rather than reacted)

I watched soldiers with mediocre shooting skills dominate engagements because they fought smart. And I saw excellent marksmen get killed because they made poor tactical decisions.

For Civilian Self-Defense:

This means that for home defense or concealed carry, your training should emphasize:

  1. Threat recognition: Identifying danger before it escalates
  2. Avoidance: Not being there when violence happens
  3. De-escalation: Talking your way out when possible
  4. Cover usage: Not standing in the open like you're on a square range
  5. Decision-making: Legal and tactical implications of using force

Marksmanship still matters—you need to hit what you're aiming at. But if that's ALL your training focuses on, you're preparing for only one small piece of the self-defense puzzle.

Lesson 5: Training Must Respect Reality

Too many firearms instructors teach what I call "square range fantasies"—techniques that work great on a static range with paper targets but have little connection to real defensive scenarios.

Examples I saw fail in Ukraine:

  • "Always use a two-handed grip" - Sometimes you need your other hand to open a door, hold a flashlight, or steady yourself
  • "Always achieve perfect sight alignment" - Sometimes you need to shoot from retention at extreme close range
  • "Reload behind cover" - Sometimes you can't get to cover and standing in the open with an empty gun is worse
  • "Shoot until the threat stops" - In low light or confusion, you might not even know when the threat has stopped

These aren't bad principles—they're good guidelines for ideal situations. But defensive encounters are rarely ideal. Training that only prepares you for perfect conditions leaves you unprepared for reality.

How I Train Differently:

My courses include:

  • One-handed shooting: Both strong and support hand, because you might be injured or holding something
  • Awkward positions: On your back, seated, kneeling, behind barriers
  • Low-light conditions: Because most defensive encounters happen in low light
  • Movement: You won't be standing still in a gunfight
  • Close quarters: Most civilian defensive shootings happen at under 7 yards
  • Malfunctions: Your gun WILL malfunction at the worst possible moment

Does this make training harder? Yes. Does it look less "cool" than standing still and punching perfect groups at 25 yards? Absolutely. But it prepares you for reality, not fantasy.

Lesson 6: Mental Preparation Matters More Than Physical Skills

The soldiers who struggled most in Ukraine weren't the ones with poor shooting skills—they were the ones who weren't mentally prepared for violence.

I saw fighters hesitate at critical moments because they had never seriously considered the reality of using lethal force. I watched soldiers second-guess themselves in situations where decisiveness was required. I saw good people paralyzed by the moral weight of defensive force.

This isn't a failure of character. It's a failure of training that focused on physical skills while ignoring the psychological reality of violence.

The Question You Must Answer

Before you ever carry a firearm for self-defense, you must answer this question: "Am I prepared to use lethal force to protect my own life or the lives of my family?" If you haven't seriously grappled with that question, your hesitation in a critical moment could cost you everything.

What This Means for Your Training:

Effective firearms training must address:

  • The psychological reality of violence: What it feels like, how your body reacts
  • The moral and legal implications: When force is justified, when it isn't
  • Decision-making under pressure: How to think clearly when your brain is flooded with adrenaline
  • The aftermath: Legal, emotional, and psychological consequences of defensive force

I incorporate these discussions into my courses because the best defensive encounter is the one you avoid, and if you can't avoid it, you need to be mentally prepared to do what's necessary to protect yourself and your loved ones.

Lesson 7: You Don't Rise to the Occasion—You Fall to Your Training

This is perhaps the most important lesson of all. Under stress, you will not perform at your best. You will not "rise to the occasion" and suddenly become a better shooter than you are in practice.

You will default to your training level—or below it.

In Ukraine, I watched this principle play out hundreds of times. Soldiers performed in combat exactly as they had trained, minus a significant performance degradation from stress. The ones who had trained to a high standard still performed acceptably under stress. The ones who had barely trained? They struggled or failed.

"In combat, you don't rise to the occasion. You fall to the level of your training. Train accordingly."

What This Means for You:

If you can barely draw your pistol and hit a target at 7 yards on a calm range day, you will not be able to do it reliably in a home invasion at 3 AM. Your stress-degraded performance will be far worse.

This is why serious training matters. Not because it makes you cool or tactical, but because it builds the buffer between your training performance and your stressed performance that might save your life.
